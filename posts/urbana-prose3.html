Purposeful Living

January 8, 2026 · Blog

A few nights ago, I was having trouble falling asleep. Partly because my brain was too active, going to bed right after my first doomscroll of the year (not my best moment).

But mostly because I was afraid I would die.

After lying down in bed around 1 am, I started to hear strange sounds outside my window. It sounded like multiple high-pitched squeaks and barks coalesced on the street outside my open window. As my brain does, I could only imagine the worst possible situation — that someone evil was out there doing something… evil. The police sirens on the next street did not help my imagination.

I started being concerned about my open window. There is a bug screen, right? Was my window locked? Could it be opened? Could bug screens be opened from the outside?

As a feeling of anxiety crept up into my chest, I had a sudden realization: I cannot die now — I need more time.

This thought surprised me, because for months I had been questioning my life. I never wanted to die, but I was okay with it happening. In the past, when I became paranoid before sleeping due to the dark or due to suspicious sounds, I wasn’t too worried about it. The difference wasn’t how concerning the sounds were, though. Rather, it was that something had shifted within me a few days earlier.

From December 27th to midnight on January 1st, I attended a conference in Phoenix called Urbana. Urbana is a major Christian conference that takes place every three years, focusing on global missions and missional living. Through Urbana, I was reminded of my identity and given a purpose, one that reframed my life in context.

I heard that there would be people with various educational and professional backgrounds, and I wanted to see how God’s plan for them could translate into what my own future holds. But I certainly didn’t expect to find answers to the question I had been quietly holding in my heart.

Throughout the conference’s sermons, seminars, and stories, I was reminded of two truths:

I. God created us uniquely and purposefully, at the right time.

Lisa Pak, Global Strategist for Finishing the Task, spoke on the book of Esther. In chapter 4 of Esther’s story, Mordecai—Esther’s fatherly figure—convinces Esther to stand and act against the king, and calls her out of her comfort zone as the queen.

Mordecai said, “For if you remain silent at this time, relief and deliverance for the Jews will arise from another place, but you and your father’s family will perish. And who knows but that you have come to your royal position for such a time as this?” (Esther 4:14 NIV).

Pak pointed out that the context—the circumstances, situations, and liabilities—in which Esther is born is used by God to save the Jews. Esther’s purpose and role in Jewish history came directly from her identity as a Jew and her living situation. She emphasized that there is a reason you were born for such a time as this, and it’s important to understand that your purpose is in the context of the times in which you were born.

Additionally, Jon Tyson, senior pastor of the Church of the City New York, said something that really resonated with me. He said, “Don’t let human plans of others rob you of your trust in God.”

In an age and culture of comparisons where it seems like everyone else is ahead of you, doing better than you, Tyson pointed out the dissonance between the success of one and the failure of another, challenging my thoughts and assumptions of what I am supposed to achieve.

At the same time, Urbana continuously reminded me of who I am and how I was created. At the conference’s registration, we took TruCenter’s faith-based assessment to catch our top motivations. And although it was just a test, the results drew out my dreams, interests, and inner desires that I previously struggled to put into words. Some of the aspects of myself that I saw as weaknesses were just innate things that drive me toward fulfillment.
